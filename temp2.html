Using national census data to understand the Australian science workforce and drivers for curriculum design

ABSTRACT
The importance of science for national innovation and competition is recognised internationally, and drives concerns about the adequacy of national STEM (science, technology, engineering and mathematics) workforces.  It is known that many Australian science graduates do not work in a science occupation in the longer term.  The makeup of the Australian science workforce is less well known.  Rational education design dictates that science curricula should be based on a realistic representation of the actual practice of Australian science graduates.  The latest Australian national census data are analysed to: identify where Bachelor of Science graduates work; identify who works in science occupations; and reveal the complexity of the Australian science workforce.  The Australian science workforce is comprised of people with a wide range of educational qualifications, and, even immediately post-graduation, many Australian science graduates pursue non-science occupations.  Beyond curriculum models that aim to prepare graduates for careers in research and industrial science practice, actual Australian science occupational outcomes point to the value of curricula that also address career options in non-science STEM occupations, non-STEM occupations, and the development of enterprise/entrepreneurial skills that would assist graduates to create their own businesses and employment.

Keywords: science education; curriculum design; occupational outcomes; census data
