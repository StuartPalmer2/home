A broader conception of work-integrated learning for engineering and science


In Australia, for decades, there has been a requirement for 12 weeks of ‘work experience’ for graduate membership of the engineering profession, and for many years most Australian engineering schools have subsumed this industry placement as a hurdle requirement for graduation.  More recently, some engineering schools have framed this industry placement as a work-integrated learning (WIL) experience that is assessed for course credit.  Without external accreditation, WIL in tertiary science courses has historically been less common, although some WIL opportunities, including for course credit, have never-the-less existed.  In 2015, the Office of the Chief Scientist recommended that all Australian undergraduate science programs include a work placement.  In the case of both engineering and science, key aims of such WIL placements are to develop a deeper understanding of discipline practice, and to enhance graduate employability.


The Australian census and national graduate outcomes data reveal a stark reality for many graduates of engineering and science university courses.  Over the last decade, graduate employment in these disciplines has declined.  Graduates in work are also less likely to be working in the discipline area that they studied.  It is not merely that we need to produce ‘better’ graduate engineers and scientists.  The census also shows that there are far fewer professional job roles available than there are graduates.  Many Australian engineering and science graduates must work outside of their discipline if they wish to work at all.  There is also existing research internationally that highlights that not all students enrolled in engineering and science courses intend to pursue careers in those disciplines.

WIL is often framed as an opportunity for students to apply discipline-specific knowledge in a workplace context.  There have been many calls to make engineering and science curricula more ‘authentic’.  The actual occupational outcomes for engineering and science graduates suggest that an authentic undergraduate curriculum is one that deliberately addresses graduate employment outcomes beyond the profession in its: philosophy and vision; student recruitment; intended learning outcomes; learning designs; curriculum content; assessment; and, most especially in its employability studies, including WIL.  A broader conception of WIL in engineering and science is required.

This chapter addresses the aims of the publication through:
- highlighting the enduring value of WIL in engineering and science education;
- analysing the latest Australian data on graduate occupational outcomes for engineering and science;
- recommending changes to engineering and science curricula relating to WIL; and
- proposing a research agenda to evaluate the impact of these changes.
