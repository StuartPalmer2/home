How to succeed in your final-year project

Your final-year project is the single largest piece of work you will complete as an undergraduate, the biggest item in the calculation of honours and one of the most important things potential employers will look at.  You should plan to achieve the best result possible.

Start early - think about possible project topics before SEJ343, discuss your ideas with potential supervisors and industrial partners.

Start early - commence your project work as soon as you can after confirming your topic and supervisor.

Start early - write your final report as you go, start with an empty document with only the headings of the required sections, and fill it up as your project progresses - don't leave your write-up until the end.

Follow the guidelines given in the course notes and the unit guide - for your project brief, proposal, interim reports, oral presentation and final report.

Always re-use your work - any work in your brief should be re-used in your proposal, and any work in your proposal should be re-used in your final report.

Stick to your schedule - for your proposal you will develop a project schedule, display it somewhere prominently and check each week that you are on track - if not, take action to regain progress, if this is not possible see your supervisor as soon as possible.

Keep busy - sometimes progress in one area is delayed, don't wait, do something else - use the time to write up everything you've done so far.

Keep in touch with your supervisor - keep your supervisor informed of progress and/or any problems - don't 'surprise' your supervisor with your problems when it is too late to take any action.

Don't confuse your project and the unit requirements - for your project (particularly if you're an off-campus student), you will be doing certain work, this work is important and is the basis for your final-year project, but you need to keep in mind there is much more required to complete the project unit - apart from the project work you are demonstrating your skills in project planning, written and oral communication, research and literature review, experimental design, analysis of data, etc.  The 'significance' of the technical work you complete for your project is only one of the criterion upon which your final project report will be assessed.

You're telling a story - For both your project proposal and your final report you will find there is a prescribed format for presentation, it is not arbitrary, it is designed to provide the reader with a logical sequence that tells a story, for example in the final report you have:

  • Introduction: the background and rationale for the project, including specific aims/outcomes;
  • Literature Review: a critical review of the related literature that provides general and specific information that is the foundation for the chosen project methodology;
  • Methodology: what you actually did and why you did it, such that someone could reproduce your work if they had to (should make sense in light of the information presented in the literature review);
  • Results: what you found/produced/discovered/etc;
  • Discussion: a critical analysis of the results/outcomes;
  • Conclusion: tying the whole thing up by comparing the results with the originally stated aims, i.e., how successful was the project?

Do more than the bare minimum - your final-year project is not the place to do just what you have to do to pass, this is the place where you should aim for the best result - if you go the extra mile, you will reap the rewards.
